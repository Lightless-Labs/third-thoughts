Full report · 2026-04-14 corpus run

What the logs said, at length.

13,423 session transcripts. A battery of analyses that all finished, for once. Here's what we looked at, what showed up, what didn't hold on a second pass, and what still needs another run.

What this project is

When a person uses an AI coding agent — Claude Code, Codex, or any of the others — the whole interaction gets written to a transcript file: every user message, every model reply, the model's internal "thinking" turns where it reasons to itself, and every tool call it made along the way. Those files are where the behavior of the system actually lives. Most people skim past them. We read them in bulk and count what's in there.

The tool that does the counting is a Rust command-line program called middens — the archaeological term for a refuse heap. That's not accidental. The things people throw away are often more honest about what they did than their polished accounts. Session logs work the same way. If you want to know whether agents actually self-correct, whether they bail on promising leads too early, whether a recent rollout changed anything important, the answer has to come from the transcript, not from a press release.

This report is a write-up of the first big clean run. All 23 analyses in the battery finished on 13,423 sessions with no errors and no timeouts. That matters less as an engineering brag than as a threshold: a toolchain that only works on toy subsets tends to produce findings with suspiciously good posture. This is the first time the whole thing stayed upright on real data long enough to let the interesting problems show.

The raw transcripts themselves are not public. Real sessions contain private code, real conversations, and enough identifying detail that we're not going to publish them just because the numbers came out interesting. The tool and the methodology are open; the source data isn't. That's not ideal for replication, but it beats being careless.

How we looked at it

At the coarse level, middens takes a folder of transcripts, parses them, labels the messages by what kind they are (user turn, agent reply, tool call, tool result, internal thinking), and then runs a long list of analyses over the resulting corpus. Some of those are about sequence: what tends to come before or after what, how behavior changes over the course of a session, whether there are recurring "modes" an agent drifts through. Others are descriptive: how diverse the tool use is, how much of what the model thinks internally makes it into what the user sees, how often users step in to correct.

The most important thing we had to learn the hard way is that our corpus isn't a single clean population. It mixes fully interactive sessions (a user at a keyboard, back and forth with the agent) with background "subagent" sessions (one agent spawning another to handle a subtask with no human in the loop). Those two behave almost nothing alike. It also mixes sessions where the model's internal thinking was written to the transcript with ones where it was hidden, English sessions with non-English ones, and different rollout windows of the underlying tools.

If you don't separate those populations, you get numbers that look impressively precise and are quietly wrong — because you're effectively averaging over different phenomena and reporting the result as if it were one. We learned this by getting it wrong at least twice. The findings below are all scoped to an explicit population; when the scope matters for interpretation, we call it out.

One other note on measurement. Detecting a "correction" in a transcript — a user telling the agent it went off the rails — looks simple until you try to do it with keyword matching. That approach performs badly on subagent sessions, where tool-result artifacts can look like user pushback if you squint at the wrong substring. The current classifier checks the actual structure of the message (what kind of content block it is) before falling back on lexical signals, and it was validated at 98% accuracy. That doesn't make every correction number perfect, but it moves the work out of the "grep and vibes" era.

What we found

1. Agents think worried thoughts, but rarely say them

Models with visible internal thinking think a lot. They produce about 700 tokens of internal reasoning for every token of user-facing text — an enormous ratio. So a reasonable question to ask is: how much of what they think privately do they also say in public?

We took a list of words that signal risk, hedging, or worry — "this might break," "I'm not sure," "this could fail," that family — and counted them in the internal thinking and the user-facing reply for every turn where both were available. Then we asked what fraction of the worry words in the thinking also appear in the reply.

Across 4,518 visible-thinking English sessions, we observed 31,679 of those worry words in the thinking. Two of them made it into the user-facing text. That's 99.99% suppression, not 100%. We'll keep saying 99.99% because the two leaks are real, and a project that rounds near-total to total is a project that eventually starts believing its own press.

The more tentative claim that doesn't survive this analysis is "more internal thinking leads to better outcomes." In the causality tests, message length and the presence of corrections help predict how much thinking the model will do; the amount of thinking does not, in turn, predict whether the session ends well. Thinking appears to be a response to complexity rather than a cause of success.

2. Probably a "drifting" state before users step in — but smaller than we said

A hidden Markov model (specifically a Hidden Semi-Markov Model, which also learns how long each state tends to last) fit five recurring "modes" across 495,561 agent turns. One of those modes, loosely describable as "minimal output — agent has gone quiet," shows up more often in the turns just before a user corrects the agent than elsewhere.

On this clean run, the effect is about 2.15× — meaningful but modest. An earlier run claimed 24.6×, which would have been huge. We traced that number to contamination: a batch of around 1,820 near-empty sessions in the corpus (all zero tool calls, all carrying the same "queue-operation" marker) that were weird enough to warp the state model all on their own. Once their influence is accounted for, the effect shrinks but doesn't vanish.

The honest status for this one is provisional. The direction replicates across runs. The size of the effect doesn't, yet. A 2.15× lift is "there might be a useful early-warning signal here." A contaminated 24.6× lift is "we measured an artifact of the corpus and called it psychology." The first is worth keeping and checking again. The second was the kind of thing you retract.

3. Agents give up on promising leads

Ecologists have a well-known result, the marginal value theorem, that predicts when a forager should leave a patch and go look for another one. Roughly: stay as long as the expected future return from this patch is better than the average return you'd get elsewhere. It applies to bees, to birds, and — slightly awkwardly — to an agent deciding how long to keep reading a file or probing a codebase area before bouncing somewhere else.

By that benchmark, zero sessions in our corpus get it right. Every session we looked at fails the test. Agents spend about 1.5 turns on average in a "patch" before moving on, jump between around six patches per session, and return to ones they already abandoned about 37% of the time. Their explore-to-exploit ratio sits around 2.6 when the theoretically efficient behavior would put it closer to parity.

There's a wrinkle worth naming. Sessions where users step in heavily can look better on the narrow patch metric, because the user's corrections effectively concentrate the agent on one spot. That doesn't rescue the overall result; it just shows that human intervention can make the local metric prettier while the session as a whole is still struggling. The big picture is: left alone, agents leave value on the table by leaving too soon.

4. Corrections happen near the start, or not at all

Most sessions never contain a user correction — only about 18% do. Of the ones that do, the correction almost always lands in the first third of the session. The per-turn correction rate in the opening third is about 0.068. In the middle third it drops to 0.014. In the final third it's 0.019.

It's tempting to read that as "agents don't get worse the longer you use them." This measure doesn't actually support that, and this is exactly where the "user didn't correct" proxy for success starts leaking. A low correction rate in the middle and final thirds is consistent with "the session is fine by then," but it's equally consistent with the user having given up, stopped paying attention, or quietly moved the goalposts. A separate survival analysis restricted to interactive sessions does surface a within-session degradation signal. So the honest reading of this chart is narrower: corrections cluster near the start. Whether sessions stay healthy after that is a different question, and this metric doesn't answer it.

This is also a different question again from whether agent behavior drifts over calendar time across rollouts — a model update can absolutely make things worse in ways this measure doesn't capture. Within-session degradation and across-version degradation are separate phenomena. The corpus can speak to both, but not with the same number.

A survival model on the same data points the same direction but with caveats worth being explicit about. Subagent sessions show a 3.28× correction-hazard, which is itself suspicious — a subagent has no human in the loop to step in. What that number almost certainly captures is the parent agent sending a follow-up that lexically resembles a correction, plus residual classifier false positives on tool-result content that still sneak past the structural check. We flag it; we don't treat it as a behavioral finding about subagents being fragile. The visible-thinking hazard (0.59×) is cleaner as a measurement but tangled with session difficulty: easier tasks route to visible-thinking models more often and go smoothly more often, and we can't yet separate the two. Suggestive, not settled.

5. Self-checking agents do well; planning-only ones don't

Two separate analyses end up pointing the same way. The first looks for recurring sequence shapes in the user/agent turn stream — short patterns like "user, agent, user, agent" or "user, user, agent-error." It turns out that a particular shape (the steady alternation) shows up about 14× more often in sessions that stayed smooth. Another shape (user repeating themselves, agent breaking) shows up about 7× more often in sessions that went badly.

The second analysis maps out the graph of "cognitive moves" an agent makes per session — things like forming a hypothesis, checking evidence, correcting itself, composing a plan. Smooth sessions disproportionately link "check evidence" to "correct yourself." Rough sessions disproportionately link "make a plan" and "frame the problem" to each other without the self-checking node getting much action.

The practical upshot is that it's easy to reward elaborate planning prose because it sounds organized. The corpus suggests that planning language on its own is not the useful signal. The useful signal is whether the agent interrogates its own work while it's gathering evidence. Planning without self-correction can be very articulate and still on a trajectory straight toward a user intervention.

6. Tool use is repetitive, but we're cautious about the universal version

Across the whole corpus, tool use looks clustered and monocultural. Agents self-loop on the same tool over and over: Bash to Bash to Bash (55% of runs), Read to Read to Read (69%), WebSearch self-loops at 74%, TaskCreate at 80%. Median tool diversity per session, measured as Shannon entropy, is basically zero. About 59% of sessions qualify as "tool monocultures" by a reasonable evenness threshold.

That's a real measurement, but two things keep it from being the headline it looks like. The first is Bash. "Bash → Bash → Bash" treats shelling out as a single tool, but a Bash call can be git status, then cargo test, then rg foo, then curl ... — effectively the rest of the operating system squeezed through one port. A fair version of this metric would parse the first token of each command and compute diversity over that. We haven't done it yet. Until we do, the Bash self-loop figure overstates repetition exactly where the underlying "tool" is most expressive, and the more interesting question — what are agents actually doing inside Bash, and is it as monocultural as the outer label suggests? — is still open.

The second is population. A large fraction of the corpus is subagent sessions with deliberately narrow jobs — "read these files and tell me what you found" — and a subagent that only reads files isn't behaviorally pathological; it's doing the one job it was given. So the honest reading is: repetitive tool use is abundant, some of it probably reflects real agent behavior, some of it is Bash collapsing a diverse universe into one label, and some of it is population structure. We need to pull those apart before turning any of this into a claim.

What didn't hold up

The single most useful habit this project has is retraction. One earlier claim — that making the model's thinking visible leads to fewer user corrections — did not survive a proper split of interactive versus subagent sessions. Those two populations behave so differently that mixing them created the appearance of an effect where none exists once you look separately. That claim has been retracted.

The earlier "85.5% risk suppression" number belongs in the same bucket, with a slightly different story. The phenomenon is real — the 99.99% figure above is the corrected version. The old headline wasn't arithmetically wrong; it was the wrong denominator. We'd been averaging over sessions where the model's thinking was hidden from the transcript, meaning there was nothing visible to "leak" from thinking into text in the first place. Those sessions got counted as zero leaks out of zero observations, which pulled the population average down to look much worse than the actual behavior. Restrict the measurement to sessions where the comparison is defined, and the real figure is near-total.

What this doesn't do well yet

Three limitations are worth naming up front.

First, the corpus is concentrated. About 94% of the sessions come from three projects. That means corpus-wide statistics are really weighted summaries of a small handful of environments. If a finding doesn't survive a project-level breakdown, it shouldn't be treated as a general property of AI coding agents.

Second, the "queue-operation / zero-tool-call" cluster still sits in the corpus, and it's unusually disruptive. 1,820 sessions of effectively empty behavior skew monoculture counts, warp diversity metrics, and (as noted above) explain most of the collapse in the HSMM effect size. Until those are cleanly isolated as their own stratum, any whole-corpus claim has to carry that asterisk.

Third, some of the downstream classifiers are still rough. A frustration classifier that sorts roughly 90% of messages into one bucket is a calibration failure, not a profound finding about human emotion. The convention analysis is effectively single-user for parts of the corpus, which means anything it says about "social diffusion" should be read as "this particular person's working habits carrying over between sessions." Neither of these invalidates the rest of the work. They do narrow what can honestly be said cleanly.

And the data itself, again, is private. That limits what a skeptical outsider can verify directly. The mitigation is to keep the tooling and methodology public, document the classifiers precisely, and be strict about the scope of each claim.

The rule we apply before calling something a finding

Every headline claim about thinking or text behavior in this project has to survive four splits of the corpus before we'll treat it as a finding: by session type (interactive vs subagent vs autonomous), by whether the thinking was visible or hidden, by language (English vs other), and by a temporal rollout window. If the claim holds on one but not on another, it's an intermediate observation, not a headline.

This rule exists because mixed populations have already manufactured several convincing-looking results that evaporated under a proper split. Once is an expensive lesson. Doing it twice would just be laziness with statistics attached.

What's next

The immediate analytic priority is to rerun the state-based and sequence-based findings on cleaner strata: exclude the zero-tool-call cluster, split by session type, split by project. If the pre-correction state signal survives that cleanup, the answer to "can we tell when a session is drifting?" gets a little firmer. If it doesn't, the honest answer stays "not yet."

There's also a longer list of questions we haven't really asked yet. How does behavior change across rollouts of the same underlying tool? Do different populations of users — beginners, professionals, people working in unfamiliar languages — induce visibly different agent behavior? Are there working patterns that the corpus suggests but that nobody has named yet? We don't know. The corpus is plausibly large enough to start asking. Most of the interesting work is going to be unglamorous: defining questions precisely enough to count against, and resisting the temptation to promote an intermediate observation into a headline before it's ready.

The project is young, which is probably healthy. The findings that are strong are strong partly because they already survived one round of unpleasant cleanup. The weak ones are weak in ways the data makes visible. That's roughly what you want out of this kind of work: not a finished theory of agent behavior, just a growing stack of claims that have passed at least one round of being less clever than they looked at first.
