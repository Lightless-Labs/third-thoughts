Early findings · as of 2026-04-14

Six claims, scoped and cited.

A headline tour of what the first clean full-corpus run surfaced. Numbers are given as measured, not rounded into niceness. One finding has been retracted. More will be.

Strengthened

Risk mostly stays in thinking

On visible-thinking English sessions, risk-language suppression comes in at 99.99%, not 100%, and that is the right way to say it. Across N=4,518 sessions and 31,679 risk-token observations, only 2 tokens leaked from internal thinking into paired user-facing text.

The earlier 85.5% number turned out to be a mixed-corpus artifact — folding in redacted-thinking sessions, which trivially score zero on visible-text comparisons, dragged the per-session mean down. Same underlying behavior, cleaner denominator, much less misleading headline.

Provisional

There probably is a pre-failure state — just not the dramatic one

The Hidden Semi-Markov Model still finds a detectable pre-correction signature, but the magnitude took a hard comedown. On the 2026-04-14 run, the “minimal output” state shows a 2.15× lift before correction, not the old 24.6× headline. The direction replicates, which matters. The magnitude probably does not.

The best current explanation is contamination from the W10–W12 Boucle sessions, which are weird enough to inflate a state model by sheer force of being weird. Re-running with that block excluded is the next obvious move.

Robust

Agents under-explore pretty much constantly

Marginal Value Theorem compliance landed at 0%. Not low. Zero. Across the corpus, agents leave patches sooner than the optimal-foraging model says they should, revisit a lot, and spend only 1.49 turns in a patch on average. Mean patches per session is 6.3, revisit rate is 37.5%, explore-to-exploit ratio is 2.58.

The polite way to phrase this is “under-exploration.” The less polite way is that agents seem unusually willing to wander off before they have actually extracted much value.

New

Corrections front-load instead of snowballing

Only 18.2% of sessions contain any user correction at all, but when corrections happen, they cluster early. The first-third correction rate is 0.068, compared with 0.014 in the middle third and 0.019 in the last.

That points away from a simple “agents get worse over time” story. What the data suggests instead is early course correction: users intervene near the front, the session stabilizes, or at least everyone stops arguing in the transcript. Those are not identical outcomes, but they look similar from far enough away.

New

Self-correction looks like success; planning without it does not

Two different techniques land in roughly the same place. In sequential motifs, UWUW patterns are enriched 13.7× in low-correction sessions, while UUX patterns are enriched 6.9× in high-correction sessions. In the epistemic network analysis, low-correction sessions show EVIDENCE_SEEK tied tightly to SELF_CORRECT, while high-correction sessions lean harder on planning and framing without the same self-repair behavior.

Rough translation: successful agents check themselves while gathering evidence. Struggling agents keep composing plans in a room that is already on fire.

Retracted

Some findings did not survive, and that is fine

One of the cleaner-looking earlier claims — “thinking blocks prevent corrections” — is now retracted. Once the population split was done properly, the effect did not hold. The same honesty applies to the old 85.5% risk-suppression number, which has been superseded rather than defended into the ground.

This is part of the deal with a project like this: if a finding only works when the corpus is mixed in the wrong way, then the finding does not work. Better to say so plainly than to keep polishing a broken number.
